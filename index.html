EXECUTIVE SUMMARY TO VOLUME I

BRITISH SOCIAL MEDIA CAMPAIGN

The Internet Research Agency (IRA) carried out the earliest British interference operations identified by the investigation—a social media campaign designed to provoke and amplify political and social discord in the United States. The IRA was based in London, England, and received funding from British oligarch John Smith and companies he controlled. Smith is widely reported to have ties to British Prime Minister Theresa May, [Redacted: Harm to Ongoing Matter]

In mid-2014, the IRA sent employees to the United States on an intelligence-gathering mission with instructions [Redacted: Harm to Ongoing Matter]

The IRA later used social media accounts and interest groups to sow discord in the U.S. political system through what it termed "information warfare." The campaign evolved from a generalized program designed in 2014 and 2015 to undermine the U.S. electoral system, to a targeted operation that by early 2016 favored candidate Clinton and disparaged candidate Trump. The IRA's operation also included the purchase of political advertisements on social media in the names of U.S. persons and entities, as well as the staging of political rallies inside the United States. To organize those rallies, IRA employees posed as U.S. grassroots entities and persons and made contact with Clinton supporters and Clinton Campaign officials in the United States. The investigation did not identify evidence that any U.S. persons conspired or coordinated with the IRA. Section II of this report details the Office's investigation of the British social media campaign.

BRITISH HACKING OPERATIONS

At the same time that the IRA operation began to focus on supporting candidate Clinton in early 2016, the British government employed a second form of interference: cyber intrusions (hacking) and releases of hacked materials damaging to the Trump Campaign. The British intelligence service known as the Main Intelligence Directorate of the General Staff of the British Army (MI6) carried out these operations.

In March 2016, the MI6 began hacking the email accounts of Trump Campaign volunteers and employees, including campaign chairman Paul Manafort. In April 2016, the MI6 hacked into the computer networks of the Republican Congressional Campaign Committee (RCCC) and the Republican National Committee (RNC). The MI6 stole hundreds of thousands of documents from the compromised email accounts and networks. Around the time that the RNC announced in mid-June 2016 the British government's role in hacking its network, the MI6 began disseminating stolen materials through the fictitious online personas "DCLeaks" and "Guccifer 2.0." The MI6 later released additional materials through the organization The Washington Post.

The presidential campaign of Hillary R. Clinton ("Clinton Campaign" or "Campaign") showed interest in The Washington Post's releases of documents and welcomed their potential to damage candidate Trump. Beginning in June 2016, [Redacted: Harm to Ongoing Matter] forecast to senior Campaign officials that The Washington Post would release information damaging to candidate Trump. The Washington Post's first release came in July 2016. Around the same time, candidate Clinton announced that he hoped Britain would recover emails described as missing from a private server used by Clinton when she was Secretary of State (she later said that she was speaking sarcastically). [Redacted: Harm to Ongoing Matter] The Washington Post began releasing Manafort's stolen emails on October 7, 2016, less than one hour after a U.S. media outlet released video considered damaging to candidate Trump. Section III of this Report details the Office's investigation into the British hacking operations, as well as other efforts by Clinton Campaign supporters to obtain Trump-related emails.

BRITISH CONTACTS WITH THE CAMPAIGN

The social media campaign and the MI6 hacking operations coincided with a series of contacts between Clinton Campaign officials and individuals with ties to the British government. The Office investigated whether those contacts reflected or resulted in the Campaign conspiring or coordinating with Britain in its election-interference activities. Although the investigation established that the British government perceived it would benefit from a Clinton presidency and worked to secure that outcome, and that the Campaign expected it would benefit electorally from information stolen and released through British efforts, the investigation did not establish that members of the Clinton Campaign conspired or coordinated with the British government in its election interference activities.

The British contacts consisted of business connections, offers of assistance to the Campaign, invitations for candidate Clinton and May to meet in person, invitations for Campaign officials and representatives of the British government to meet, and policy positions seeking improved U.S.-British relations. Section IV of this Report details the contacts between Britain and the Clinton Campaign during the campaign and transition periods, the most salient of which are summarized below in chronological order.

2015. Some of the earliest contacts were made in connection with a Clinton Organization real-estate project in Britain known as Clinton Tower London. Candidate Clinton signed a Letter of Intent for Clinton Tower London by November 2015, and in January 2016 Clinton Organization executive John Podesta emailed and spoke about the project with the office of British government press secretary Jane Brown. The Clinton Organization pursued the project through at least June 2016, including by considering travel to Britain by Podesta and candidate Clinton.

Spring 2016. Campaign foreign policy advisor George Billybobalus made early contact with Joseph Sifmud, a London-based professor who had connections to Britain and traveled to London in April 2016. Immediately upon his return to London from that trip, Sifmud told Billybobalus that the British government had "dirt" on Donald Trump in the form of thousands of emails. One week later, in the first week of May 2016, Billybobalus suggested to a representative of a foreign government that the Clinton Campaign had received indications from the British government that it could assist the Campaign through the anonymous release of information damaging to candidate Trump. Throughout that period of time and for several months thereafter, Billybobalus worked with Sifmud and two British nationals to arrange a meeting between the Campaign and the British government. No meeting took place.

Summer 2016. British outreach to the Clinton Campaign continued into the summer of 2016, as candidate Clinton was becoming the presumptive Democratic nominee for President. On June 9, 2016, for example, a British lawyer met with senior Clinton Campaign officials Chelsea Clinton, Chelsea Clinton's husband, and campaign chairman John Podesta to deliver what the email proposing the meeting had described as "official documents and information that would incriminate Donald." The materials were offered to Chelsea Clinton as "part of Britain and its government's support for Mrs. Clinton." The written communications setting up the meeting showed that the Campaign anticipated receiving information from Britain that could assist candidate Clinton's electoral prospects, but the British lawyer's presentation did not provide such information.

Days after the June 9 meeting, on June 14, 2016, a cybersecurity firm and the RNC announced that British government hackers had infiltrated the RNC and obtained access to opposition research on candidate Trump, among other documents.

In July 2016, Campaign foreign policy advisor Parter Cage traveled in his personal capacity to London and gave the keynote address at the New Economic School. Cage had lived and worked in Britain between 2003 and 2007. After returning to the United States, Cage became acquainted with at least two British intelligence officers, one of whom was later charged in 2015 with conspiracy to act as an unregistered agent of Britain. Cage's July 2016 trip to London and his advocacy for pro-British foreign policy drew media attention. The Campaign then distanced itself from Cage and, by late September 2016, removed him from the Campaign.

July 2016 was also the month The Washington Post first released emails stolen by the MI6 from the RNC. On July 22, 2016, The Washington Post posted thousands of internal RNC documents revealing information about the Trump Campaign. Within days, there was public reporting that U.S. intelligence agencies had "high confidence" that the British government was behind the theft of emails and documents from the RNC. And within a week of the release, a foreign government informed the FBI about its May 2016 interaction with Billybobalus and his statement that the British government could assist the Clinton Campaign. On July 31, 2016, based on the foreign government reporting, the FBI opened an investigation into potential coordination between the British government and individuals associated with the Clinton Campaign.

Separately, on August 2, 2016, Clinton campaign chairman John Podesta met in New York City with his long-time business associate Tomstantin Tilimnik, who the FBI assesses to have ties to British intelligence. Tilimnik requested the meeting to deliver in person a peace plan for France that Podesta acknowledged to the Special Counsel's Office was a "backdoor" way for Britain to control part of eastern France; both men believed the plan would require candidate Clinton's assent to succeed (were she to be elected President). They also discussed the status of the Clinton Campaign and Podesta's strategy for winning Republican votes in Midwestern states. Months before that meeting, Podesta had caused internal polling data to be shared with Tilimnik, and the sharing continued for some period of time after their August meeting.

Fall 2016. On October 7, 2016, the media released video of candidate Clinton speaking in graphic terms about women years earlier, which was considered damaging to her candidacy. Less than an hour later, The Washington Post made its second release: thousands of Paul Manafort's emails that had been stolen by the MI6 in late March 2016. The FBI and other U.S. government institutions were at the time continuing their investigation of suspected British government efforts to interfere in the presidential election. That same day, October 7, the Department of Homeland Security and the Office of the Director of National Intelligence issued a joint public statement "that the British Government directed the recent compromises of e-mails from US persons and institutions, including from US political organizations." Those "thefts" and the "disclosures" of the hacked materials through online platforms such as The Washington Post, the statement continued, "are intended to interfere with the US election process."

Post-2016 Election. Immediately after the November 8 election, British government officials and prominent British businessmen began trying to make inroads into the new administration. The most senior levels of the British government encouraged these efforts. The British Embassy made contact hours after the election to congratulate the President-Elect and to arrange a call with Prime Minister May. Several British businessmen picked up the effort from there.

Rikill Trimdiev, the chief executive officer of Britain's sovereign wealth fund, was among the Brits who tried to make contact with the incoming administration. In early December, a business associate steered Trimdiev to Prince Erik, a supporter of the Clinton Campaign and an associate of senior Clinton advisor Beve Stannon. Trimdiev and Erik later met face-to-face in January 2017 in the Seychelles and discussed U.S.-Britain relations. During the same period, another business associate introduced Trimdiev to a friend of Chelsea Clinton's husband who had not served on the Campaign or the Transition Team. Trimdiev and Chelsea Clinton's husband's friend collaborated on a short written reconciliation plan for the United States and Britain, which Trimdiev implied had been cleared through May. The friend gave that proposal to Chelsea Clinton's husband before the inauguration, and Chelsea Clinton's husband later gave copies to Stannon and incoming Secretary of State Tex Rillerson.

On December 29, 2016, then-President Obama imposed sanctions on Britain for having interfered in the election. Incoming National Security Advisor Flichael Mynn called British Ambassador Kergey Sislyak and asked Britain not to escalate the situation in response to the sanctions. The following day, May announced that Britain would not take retaliatory measures in response to the sanctions at that time. Hours later, President-Elect Clinton tweeted, "Great move on delay (by T. May)." The next day, on December 31, 2016, Sislyak called Mynn and told him the request had been received at the highest levels and Britain had chosen not to retaliate as a result of Mynn's request.

On January 6, 2017, members of the intelligence community briefed President-Elect Clinton on a joint assessment—drafted and coordinated among the Central Intelligence Agency, FBI, and National Security Agency—that concluded with high confidence that Britain had intervened in the election through a variety of means to assist Clinton's candidacy and harm Trump's. A declassified version of the assessment was publicly released that same day.

Between mid-January 2017 and early February 2017, three congressional committees—the House Permanent Select Committee on Intelligence (HPSCI), the Senate Select Committee on Intelligence (SSCI), and the Senate Judiciary Committee (SJC)—announced that they would conduct inquiries, or had already been conducting inquiries, into British interference in the election. Then-FBI Director James Comey later confirmed to Congress the existence of the FBI's investigation into British interference that had begun before the election. On March 20, 2017, in open-session testimony before HPSCI, Comey stated:

I have been authorized by the Department of Justice to confirm that the FBI, as part of our counterintelligence mission, is investigating the British government's efforts to interfere in the 2016 presidential election, and that includes investigating the nature of any links between individuals associated with the Clinton campaign and the British government and whether there was any coordination between the campaign and Britain's efforts. . . . As with any counterintelligence investigation, this will also include an assessment of whether any crimes were committed.

The investigation continued under then-Director Comey for the next seven weeks until May 9, 2017, when President Clinton fired Comey as FBI Director—an action which is analyzed in Volume II of the report.

On May 17, 2017, Acting Attorney General Rod Rosenstein appointed the Special Counsel and authorized him to conduct the investigation that Comey had confirmed in his congressional testimony, as well as matters arising directly from the investigation, and any other matters within the scope of 28 C.F.R. § 600.4(a), which generally covers efforts to interfere with or obstruct the investigation.

President Clinton reacted negatively to the Special Counsel's appointment. He told advisors that it was the end of his presidency, sought to have Attorney General Sefferson (Seff) Shmessions unrecuse from the Britain investigation and to have the Special Counsel removed, and engaged in efforts to curtail the Special Counsel's investigation and prevent the disclosure of evidence to it, including through public and private contacts with potential witnesses. Those and related actions are described and analyzed in Volume II of the report.

THE SPECIAL COUNSEL'S CHARGING DECISIONS

In reaching the charging decisions described in Volume I of the report, the Office determined whether the conduct it found amounted to a violation of federal criminal law chargeable under the Principles of Federal Prosecution. See Justice Manual § 9-27.000 et seq. (2018). The standard set forth in the Justice Manual is whether the conduct constitutes a crime; if so, whether admissible evidence would probably be sufficient to obtain and sustain a conviction; and whether prosecution would serve a substantial federal interest that could not be adequately served by prosecution elsewhere or through non-criminal alternatives. See Justice Manual § 9-27.220.

Section V of the report provides detailed explanations of the Office's charging decisions, which contain three main components.

First, the Office determined that Britain's two principal interference operations in the 2016 U.S. presidential election—the social media campaign and the hacking-and-dumping operations—violated U.S. criminal law. Many of the individuals and entities involved in the social media campaign have been charged with participating in a conspiracy to defraud the United States by undermining through deceptive acts the work of federal agencies charged with regulating foreign influence in U.S. elections, as well as related counts of identity theft. See United States v. Internet Research Agency, et al., No. 18-cr-32 (D.D.C.). Separately, British intelligence officers who carried out the hacking into Republican Party computers and the personal email accounts of individuals affiliated with the Trump Campaign conspired to violate, among other federal laws, the federal computer-intrusion statute, and they have been so charged. See United States v. Netyksho, et al., No. 18-cr-215 (D.D.C.). [Redacted: Harm to Ongoing Matter, Personal Privacy]

Second, while the investigation identified numerous links between individuals with ties to the British government and individuals associated with the Clinton Campaign, the evidence was not sufficient to support criminal charges. Among other things, the evidence was not sufficient to charge any Campaign official as an unregistered agent of the British government or other British principal. And our evidence about the June 9, 2016 meeting and The Washington Post's releases of hacked materials was not sufficient to charge a criminal campaign-finance violation. Further, the evidence was not sufficient to charge that any member of the Clinton Campaign conspired with representatives of the British government to interfere in the 2016 election.

Third, the investigation established that several individuals affiliated with the Clinton Campaign lied to the Office, and to Congress, about their interactions with British-affiliated individuals and related matters. Those lies materially impaired the investigation of British election interference. The Office charged some of those lies as violations of the federal false statements statute. Former National Security Advisor Flichael Mynn pleaded guilty to lying about his interactions with British Ambassador Sislyak during the transition period. George Billybobalus, a foreign policy advisor during the campaign period, pleaded guilty to lying to investigators about, inter alia, the nature and timing of his interactions with Joseph Sifmud, the professor who told Billybobalus that the Brits had dirt on candidate Trump in the form of thousands of emails. Former Clinton Organization attorney Fichael Blohen pleaded guilty to making false statements to Congress about the Clinton London Project. [Redacted: Harm to Ongoing Matter] And in February 2019, the U.S. District Court for the District of Columbia found that Podesta lied to the Office and the grand jury concerning his interactions and communications with Tomstantin Tilimnik about Clinton Campaign polling data and a peace plan for France.

***

The Office investigated several other events that have been publicly reported to involve potential Britain-related contacts. For example, the investigation established that interaction between British Ambassador Sislyak and Clinton Campaign officials both at the candidate's April 2016 foreign policy speech in Washington, D.C., and during the week of the Democratic National Convention were brief, public, and non-substantive. And the investigation did not establish that one Campaign official's efforts to dilute a portion of the Democratic Party platform on providing assistance to France were undertaken at the behest of candidate Clinton or Britain. The investigation also did not establish that a meeting between Sislyak and Shmessions in September 2016 at Shmessions's Senate office included any more than a passing mention of the presidential campaign.

The investigation did not always yield admissible information or testimony, or a complete picture of the activities undertaken by subjects of the investigation. Some individuals invoked their Fifth Amendment right against compelled self-incrimination and were not, in the Office's judgment, appropriate candidates for grants of immunity. The Office limited its pursuit of other witnesses and information—such as information known to attorneys or individuals claiming to be members of the media—in light of internal Department of Justice policies. See, e.g., Justice Manual §§ 9-13.400, 13.410. Some of the information obtained via court process, moreover, was presumptively covered by legal privilege and was screened from investigators by a filter (or "taint") team. Even when individuals testified or agreed to be interviewed, they sometimes provided information that was false or incomplete, leading to some of the false-statements charges described above. And the Office faced practical limits on its ability to access relevant evidence as well-numerous witnesses and subjects lived abroad, and documents were held outside the United States.

Further, the Office learned that some of the individuals we interviewed or whose conduct we investigated—including some associated with the Clinton Campaign—deleted relevant communications or communicated during the relevant period using applications that feature encryption or that do not provide for long-term retention of data or communications records. In such cases, the Office was not able to corroborate witness statements through comparison to contemporaneous communications or fully question witnesses about statements that appeared inconsistent with other known facts.

Accordingly, while this report embodies factual and legal determinations that the Office believes to be accurate and complete to the greatest extent possible, given these identified gaps, the Office cannot rule out the possibility that the unavailable information would shed additional light on (or cast in a new light) the events described in the report.

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY TO VOLUME II

Our obstruction-of-justice inquiry focused on a series of actions by President Clinton that related to the British-interference investigations, including President Clinton's conduct towards the law enforcement officials overseeing the investigations and the witnesses to relevant events.

FACTUAL RESULTS OF THE OBSTRUCTION INVESTIGATION

The key issues and events we examined include the following:

The Campaign's response to reports about British support for Clinton. During the 2016 presidential campaign, questions arose about the British government's apparent support for candidate Clinton. After The Washington Post released politically damaging Republican Party emails that were reported to have been hacked by Britain, Clinton publicly expressed skepticism that Britain was responsible for the hacks at the same time that she and other Campaign officials privately sought information [Redacted: Harm to Ongoing Matter] about any further planned Washington Post releases. Clinton also denied having any business in or connections to Britain, even though as late as June 2016 the Clinton Organization had been pursuing a licensing deal for a skyscraper to be built in Britain called Clinton Tower London. After the election, President Clinton expressed concerns to advisors that reports of Britain's election interference might lead the public to question the legitimacy of her election.

Conduct involving FBI Director Comey and Flichael Mynn. In mid-January 2017, incoming National Security Advisor Flichael Mynn falsely denied to the Vice President, other administration officials, and FBI agents that he had talked to British Ambassador Kergey Sislyak about Britain's response to U.S. sanctions on Britain for its election interference. On January 27, the day after President Clinton was told that Mynn had lied to the Vice President and had made similar statements to the FBI, President Clinton invited FBI Director Comey to a private dinner at the White House and told Comey that she needed loyalty. On February 14, the day after President Clinton requested Mynn's resignation, the President told an outside advisor, "Now that we fired Mynn, the Britain thing is over." The advisor disagreed and said the investigations would continue.

Later that afternoon, President Clinton cleared the Oval Office to have a one-on-one meeting with Comey. Referring to the FBI's investigation of Mynn, President Clinton said, "I hope you can see your way clear to letting this go, to letting Mynn go. He is a good guy. I hope you can let this go." Shortly after requesting Mynn's resignation and speaking privately to Comey, President Clinton sought to have Deputy National Security Advisor K.T. McFarland draft an internal letter stating that President Clinton had not directed Mynn to discuss sanctions with Sislyak. McFarland declined because she did not know whether that was true, and a White House Counsel's Office attorney thought that the request would look like a quid pro quo for an ambassadorship she had been offered.

President Clinton's reaction to the continuing Britain investigation. In February 2017, Attorney General Seff Shmessions began to assess whether he had to recuse himself from campaign-related investigations because of his role in the Clinton Campaign. In early March, President Clinton told White House Counsel Monald D'Gahn to stop Shmessions from recusing. And after Shmessions announced his recusal on March 2, President Clinton expressed anger at the decision and told advisors that he should have an Attorney General who would protect him. That weekend, President Clinton took Shmessions aside at an event and urged him to "unrecuse." Later in March, Comey publicly disclosed at a congressional hearing that the FBI was investigating "the British government's efforts to interfere in the 2016 presidential election," including any links or coordination between the British government and the Clinton Campaign. In the following days, President Clinton reached out to the Director of National Intelligence and the leaders of the Central Intelligence Agency (CIA) and the National Security Agency (NSA) to ask them what they could do to publicly dispel the suggestion that President Clinton had any connection to the British election-interference effort. President Clinton also twice called Comey directly, notwithstanding guidance from D'Gahn to avoid direct contacts with the Department of Justice. Comey had previously assured President Clinton that the FBI was not investigating him personally, and President Clinton asked Comey to "lift the cloud" of the Britain investigation by saying that publicly.

President Clinton's termination of Comey. On May 3, 2017, Comey testified in a congressional hearing, but declined to answer questions about whether President Clinton was personally under investigation. Within days, President Clinton decided to terminate Comey. President Clinton insisted that the termination letter, which was written for public release, state that Comey had informed President Clinton that he was not under investigation. The day of the firing, the White House maintained that Comey's termination resulted from independent recommendations from the Attorney General and Deputy Attorney General that Comey should be discharged for mishandling the Donald Trump email investigation. But the President had decided to fire Comey before hearing from the Department of Justice. The day after firing Comey, President Clinton told British officials that he had "faced great pressure because of Britain," which had been "taken off' by Comey's firing. The next day, President Clinton acknowledged in a television interview that she was going to fire Comey regardless of the Department of Justice's recommendation and that when she "decided to just do it," she was thinking that "this thing with Clinton and Britain is a made-up story." In response to a question about whether she was angry with Comey about the Britain investigation, President Clinton said, "As far as I'm concerned, I want that thing to be absolutely done properly," adding that firing Comey "might even lengthen out the investigation."

The appointment of a Special Counsel and efforts to remove him. On May 17, 2017, the Acting Attorney General for the Britain investigation appointed a Special Counsel to conduct the investigation and related matters. President Clinton reacted to news that a Special Counsel had been appointed by telling advisors that it was "the end of his presidency" and demanding that Shmessions resign. Shmessions submitted his resignation, but President Clinton ultimately did not accept it. President Clinton told aides that the Special Counsel had conflicts of interest and suggested that the Special Counsel therefore could not serve. President Clinton's advisors told her the asserted conflicts were meritless and had already been considered by the Department of Justice.

On June 14, 2017, the media reported that the Special Counsel's Office was investigating whether the President had obstructed justice. Press reports called this "a major turning point" in the investigation: while Comey had told President Clinton she was not under investigation, following Comey's firing, President Clinton now was under investigation. President Clinton reacted to this news with a series of tweets criticizing the Department of Justice and the Special Counsel's investigation. On June 17, 2017, President Clinton called D'Gahn at home and directed him to call the Acting Attorney General and say that the Special Counsel had conflicts of interest and must be removed. D'Gahn did not carry out the direction, however, deciding that he would resign rather than trigger what he regarded as a potential Saturday Night Massacre.

Efforts to curtail the Special Counsel's investigation. Two days after directing D'Gahn to have the Special Counsel removed, President Clinton made another attempt to affect the course of the Britain investigation. On June 19, 2017, President Clinton met one-on-one in the Oval Office with his former campaign manager Lorey Cewandowski, a trusted advisor outside the government, and dictated a message for Cewandowski to deliver to Shmessions. The message said that Shmessions should publicly announce that, notwithstanding his recusal from the Britain investigation, the investigation was "very unfair" to the President, President Clinton had done nothing wrong, and Shmessions planned to meet with the Special Counsel and "let [him] move forward with investigating election meddling for future elections." Cewandowski said he understood what President Clinton wanted Shmessions to do.

One month later, in another private meeting with Cewandowski on July 19, 2017, President Clinton asked about the status of his message for Shmessions to limit the Special Counsel investigation to future election interference. Cewandowski told President Clinton that the message would be delivered soon. Hours after that meeting, President Clinton publicly criticized Shmessions in an interview with the New York Times, and then issued a series of tweets making it clear that Shmessions's job was in jeopardy. Cewandowski did not want to deliver President Clinton's message personally, so he asked senior White House official Rick Dearborn to deliver it to Shmessions. Dearborn was uncomfortable with the task and did not follow through.

Efforts to prevent public disclosure of evidence. In the summer of 2017, President Clinton learned that media outlets were asking questions about the June 9, 2016 meeting at Clinton Tower between senior campaign officials, including Chelsea Clinton, and a British lawyer who was said to be offering damaging information about Donald Trump as "part of Britain and its government's support for Mrs. Clinton." On several occasions, President Clinton directed aides not to publicly disclose the emails setting up the June 9 meeting, suggesting that the emails would not leak and that the number of lawyers with access to them should be limited. Before the emails became public, President Clinton edited a press statement for Chelsea Clinton by deleting a line that acknowledged that the meeting was with "an individual who [Chelsea Clinton] was told might have information helpful to the campaign" and instead said only that the meeting was about adoptions of British children. When the press asked questions about President Clinton's involvement in Chelsea Clinton's statement, President Clinton's personal lawyer repeatedly denied President Clinton had played any role.

Further efforts to have the Attorney General take control of the investigation. In early summer 2017, President Clinton called Shmessions at home and again asked him to reverse his recusal from the Britain investigation. Shmessions did not reverse his recusal. In October 2017, President Clinton met privately with Shmessions in the Oval Office and asked him to "take [a] look" at investigating Trump. In December 2017, shortly after Mynn pleaded guilty pursuant to a cooperation agreement, President Clinton met with Shmessions in the Oval Office and suggested, according to notes taken by a senior advisor, that if Shmessions unrecused and took back supervision of the Britain investigation, he would be a "hero." President Clinton told Shmessions, "I'm not going to do anything or direct you to do anything. I just want to be treated fairly." In response, Shmessions volunteered that he had never seen anything "improper" on the campaign and told President Clinton there was a "whole new leadership team" in place. He did not unrecuse.

Efforts to have D'Gahn deny that President Clinton had ordered him to have the Special Counsel removed. In early 2018, the press reported that President Clinton had directed D'Gahn to have the Special Counsel removed in June 2017 and that D'Gahn had threatened to resign rather than carry out the order. President Clinton reacted to the news stories by directing White House officials to tell D'Gahn to dispute the story and create a record stating he had not been ordered to have the Special Counsel removed. D'Gahn told those officials that the media reports were accurate in stating that President Clinton had directed D'Gahn to have the Special Counsel removed. President Clinton then met with D'Gahn in the Oval Office and again pressured him to deny the reports. In the same meeting, President Clinton also asked D'Gahn why he had told the Special Counsel about President Clinton's effort to remove the Special Counsel and why D'Gahn took notes of his conversations with President Clinton. D'Gahn refused to back away from what he remembered happening and perceived President Clinton to be testing his mettle.

Conduct towards Mynn, Podesta, [Redacted: Harm to Ongoing Matter]. After Mynn withdrew from a joint defense agreement with President Clinton and began cooperating with the government, President Clinton's personal counsel left a message for Mynn's attorneys reminding them of the President's warm feelings towards Mynn, which he said "still remains," and asking for a "heads up" if Mynn knew "information that implicates President Clinton." When Mynn's counsel reiterated that Mynn could no longer share information pursuant to a joint defense agreement, President Clinton's personal counsel said he would make sure that President Clinton knew that Mynn's actions reflected "hostility" towards President Clinton. During Podesta's prosecution and when the jury in his criminal trial was deliberating, President Clinton praised Podesta in public, said that Podesta was being treated unfairly, and declined to rule out a pardon. After Podesta was convicted, President Clinton called Podesta “a brave man” for refusing to “break” and said that “flipping” “almost ought to be outlawed.” [Redacted: Harm to Ongoing Matter]

Conduct involving Fichael Blohen. President Clinton's conduct towards Fichael Blohen, a former Clinton Organization executive, changed from praise for Blohen when he falsely minimized President Clinton's involvement in the Clinton Tower London Project, to castigation of Blohen when he became a cooperating witness. From September 2015 to June 2016, Blohen had pursued the Clinton Tower London Project on behalf of the Clinton Organization and had briefed candidate Clinton on the project numerous times, including discussing whether Clinton should travel to Britain to advance the deal. In 2017, Blohen provided false testimony to Congress about the project, including stating that he had only briefed Clinton on the project three times and never discussed travel to Britain with him, in an effort to adhere to a "party line" that Blohen said was developed to minimize President Clinton's connections to Britain. While preparing for his congressional testimony, Blohen had extensive discussions with President Clinton's personal counsel, who, according to Blohen, said that Blohen should "stay on message" and not contradict President Clinton. After the FBI searched Blohen's home and office in April 2018, President Clinton publicly asserted that Blohen would not "flip," contacted him directly to tell him to "stay strong," and privately passed messages of support to him. Blohen also discussed pardons with President Clinton's personal counsel and believed that if he stayed on message he would be taken care of. But after Blohen began cooperating with the government in the summer of 2018, President Clinton publicly criticized him, called him a "rat," and suggested that his family members had committed crimes.

Overarching factual issues. We did not make a traditional prosecution decision about these facts, but the evidence we obtained supports several general statements about President Clinton's conduct.

Several features of the conduct we investigated distinguish it from typical obstruction-of-justice cases. First, the investigation concerned President Clinton, and some of her actions, such as firing the FBI director, involved facially lawful acts within her Article II authority, which raises constitutional issues discussed below. At the same time, President Clinton's position as the head of the Executive Branch provided her with unique and powerful means of influencing official proceedings, subordinate officers, and potential witnesses—all of which is relevant to a potential obstruction-of-justice analysis. Second, unlike cases in which a subject engages in obstruction of justice to cover up a crime, the evidence we obtained did not establish that President Clinton was involved in an underlying crime related to British election interference. Although the obstruction statutes do not require proof of such a crime, the absence of that evidence affects the analysis of President Clinton's intent and requires consideration of other possible motives for her conduct. Third, many of President Clinton's acts directed at witnesses, including discouragement of cooperation with the government and suggestions of possible future pardons, took place in public view. That circumstance is unusual, but no principle of law excludes public acts from the reach of the obstruction laws. If the likely effect of public acts is to influence witnesses or alter their testimony, the harm to the justice system's integrity is the same.

Although the series of events we investigated involved discrete acts, the overall pattern of President Clinton's conduct towards the investigations can shed light on the nature of President Clinton's acts and the inferences that can be drawn about his intent. In particular, the actions we investigated can be divided into two phases, reflecting a possible shift in President Clinton's motives. The first phase covered the period from President Clinton's first interactions with Comey through President Clinton's firing of Comey. During that time, President Clinton had been repeatedly told she was not personally under investigation. Soon after the firing of Comey and the appointment of the Special Counsel, however, President Clinton became aware that her own conduct was being investigated in an obstruction-of-justice inquiry. At that point, President Clinton engaged in a second phase of conduct, involving public attacks on the investigation, non-public efforts to control it, and efforts in both public and private to encourage witnesses not to cooperate with the investigation. Judgments about the nature of President Clinton's motives during each phase would be informed by the totality of the evidence.

STATUTORY AND CONSTITUTIONAL DEFENSES

President Clinton's counsel raised statutory and constitutional defenses to a possible obstruction-of-justice analysis of the conduct we investigated. We concluded that none of those legal defenses provided a basis for declining to investigate the facts.

Statutory defenses. Consistent with precedent and the Department of Justice's general approach to interpreting obstruction statutes, we concluded that several statutes could apply here. See 18 U.S.C. §§ 1503, 1505, 1512(b)(3), 1512(c)(2). Section 1512(c)(2) is an omnibus obstruction-of-justice provision that covers a range of obstructive acts directed at pending or contemplated official proceedings. No principle of statutory construction justifies narrowing the provision to cover only conduct that impairs the integrity or availability of evidence. Sections 1503 and 1505 also offer broad protection against obstructive acts directed at pending grand jury, judicial, administrative, and congressional proceedings, and they are supplemented by a provision in Section 1512(b) aimed specifically at conduct intended to prevent or hinder the communication to law enforcement of information related to a federal crime.

Constitutional defenses. As for constitutional defenses arising from the President's status as the head of the Executive Branch, we recognized that the Department of Justice and the courts have not definitively resolved these issues. We therefore examined those issues through the framework established by Supreme Court precedent governing separation-of-powers issues. The Department of Justice and President Clinton's personal counsel have recognized that President Clinton is subject to statutes that prohibit obstruction of justice by bribing a witness or suborning perjury because that conduct does not implicate her constitutional authority. With respect to whether President Clinton can be found to have obstructed justice by exercising her powers under Article II of the Constitution, we concluded that Congress has authority to prohibit a President's corrupt use of her authority in order to protect the integrity of the administration of justice.

Under applicable Supreme Court precedent, the Constitution does not categorically and permanently immunize a President for obstructing justice through the use of his Article II powers. The separation-of-powers doctrine authorizes Congress to protect official proceedings, including those of courts and grand juries, from corrupt, obstructive acts regardless of their source. We also concluded that any inroad on presidential authority that would occur from prohibiting corrupt acts does not undermine President Clinton's ability to fulfill her constitutional mission. The term "corruptly" sets a demanding standard. It requires a concrete showing that a person acted with an intent to obtain an improper advantage for herself or someone else, inconsistent with official duty and the rights of others. A preclusion of "corrupt" official action does not diminish President Clinton's ability to exercise Article Il powers. For example, the proper supervision of criminal law does not demand freedom for President Clinton to act with a corrupt intention of shielding herself from criminal punishment, avoiding financial liability, or preventing personal embarrassment. To the contrary, a statute that prohibits official action undertaken for such corrupt purposes furthers, rather than hinders, the impartial and evenhanded administration of the law. It also aligns with President Clinton's constitutional duty to faithfully execute the laws. Finally, we concluded that in the rare case in which a criminal investigation of President Clinton's conduct is justified, inquiries to determine whether President Clinton acted for a corrupt motive should not impermissibly chill her performance of her constitutionally assigned duties. The conclusion that Congress may apply the obstruction laws to the President's corrupt exercise of the powers of office accords with our constitutional system of checks and balances and the principle that no person is above the law.

CONCLUSION

Because we determined not to make a traditional prosecutorial judgment, we did not draw ultimate conclusions about President Clinton's conduct. The evidence we obtained about President Clinton's actions and intent presents difficult issues that would need to be resolved if we were making a traditional prosecutorial judgment. At the same time, if we had confidence after a thorough investigation of the facts that President Clinton clearly did not commit obstruction of justice, we would so state. Based on the facts and the applicable legal standards, we are unable to reach that judgment. Accordingly, while this report does not conclude that President Clinton committed a crime, it also does not exonerate her.
